Software Supply Chain Security in 2024–2025: Risks, Strategies, and Trends

An overview of the evolving landscape of software supply chain security.

Overview of Current Supply Chain Security Risks

Modern software is rarely built from scratch – it’s assembled from numerous components and services. This complexity has expanded the software supply chain attack surface, which attackers now aggressively exploit. Supply chain attacks have surged dramatically, becoming one of the most significant cyber threats today. Below are key categories of current risks:

Malicious or Compromised Open-Source Packages

Public package repositories (npm, PyPI, etc.) are flooded with malicious uploads. In 2023 alone, security researchers found ~11,200 unique malicious packages on npm, PyPI, and RubyGems – a 1300% increase since 2020. Attackers use techniques like typosquatting (publishing packages with names similar to popular ones) and dependency hijacking to trick developers into importing trojaned libraries. Many of these malicious packages function as infostealers or backdoors, stealing credentials and data. The barrier to entry for such attacks has lowered; even cybercriminal groups now leverage open-source malware to compromise downstream users.

Exploiting Vulnerabilities in Widely Used Components

Unpatched security flaws in ubiquitous libraries or software can be just as devastating as intentional malware. History has shown this with incidents like Log4j’s Log4Shell flaw (2021) and the 2017 Equifax breach via an Apache Struts bug. More recently, in 2023, the MOVEit file transfer software had a zero-day vulnerability that was exploited globally by a ransomware group. The attacks on MOVEit exposed personal data of over 62 million people and hit dozens of federal agencies, illustrating how a single flaw in a third-party product can cascade across healthcare, government, and more. Such events underscore the risk of relying on software (open-source or proprietary) without diligent patch management and visibility into one’s dependencies.

Compromise of Software Build and Update Infrastructure

Attackers target the software at its source – the developers or build systems – to insert malicious code into legitimate products. This can occur through phishing or malware that steals developer credentials, or by directly breaching a vendor’s network. A high-profile example was the 3CX incident (2023), where North Korean actors trojanized a popular VoIP desktop app by compromising 3CX’s build process. The attackers first breached another software supplier (Trading Technologies’ X_TRADER), then leveraged that foothold to infect 3CX’s signed software with malware, which was subsequently delivered as an automatic update to thousands of 3CX customers. This “hack of the developer” approach – also seen in the SolarWinds 2020 attack – means that even software obtained from trusted sources can be weaponized if the vendor’s pipeline is undermined.

Attacks on Trusted Service Providers and Infrastructure

Beyond code itself, adversaries also target the services that organizations trust. The Okta breach (2023) highlighted this risk: attackers stole an admin credential to Okta’s customer support portal and used it to access sensitive client data. Because Okta is an identity provider to thousands of organizations, a compromise of its systems can have downstream effects on many customers at once. Cloud platforms, managed service providers, CI/CD services, and other third-party platforms are attractive targets (“one hack, many victims”). Likewise, compromising code signing certificates or update servers allows malware to be distributed under the guise of legitimate, signed software – as happened when North Korea’s Lazarus Group breached a multimedia software firm in late 2023 and signed a malicious installer with the company’s own certificate.

Lack of Visibility and Governance in the Software Supply Chain

Many organizations struggle to even inventory their software components and suppliers. One study found modern applications are composed of 70–90% open-source code, yet only ~20% of organizations have full visibility into their software supply chain. This opacity is a risk in itself – you can’t secure what you don’t know you are using. Hidden dependencies may harbor unpatched bugs or malicious code. In 2023, over 40,000 developer secrets (API keys, credentials, etc.) were found leaked across package repositories, underscoring how mismanagement in the supply chain can expose sensitive assets. Limited visibility and fragmented responsibility make it hard to assess upstream component trustworthiness, slowing response when an incident occurs.

Bottom line: Software supply chain attacks exploit the inherent trust and complexity in how software is built and obtained. Whether through injecting malicious components, exploiting known flaws, or hijacking vendor systems, attackers aim to turn the very software we rely on into a Trojan horse. As one report aptly stated, “software supply chains represent the largest unaddressed attack surface” in many enterprises today. No organization – whether consuming open-source or proprietary products – is immune to these risks.

Mitigation Strategies, Tools, and Frameworks

Faced with these dangers, the industry has responded with a robust set of mitigation strategies and tools to secure the software supply chain. Effective defense requires a combination of process improvements, security tools, and adoption of emerging frameworks:

Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)

An SBOM is essentially an ingredient list for software, enumerating all components (open-source, commercial, libraries, modules) in a standardized format. SBOMs bring much-needed transparency – allowing organizations to quickly assess if a newly disclosed vulnerability (e.g. in an open-source library) exists in any software they use. Following U.S. Executive Order 14028 (2021), SBOMs have become a cornerstone of supply chain security initiatives. Tools like CycloneDX and SPDX support SBOM generation. In practice, using SBOMs means that, for example, when a flaw like Log4Shell is announced, a company can search its SBOMs to find every occurrence of Log4j in its environment and prioritize patches. SBOM adoption is growing – thousands of SBOMs are now published by software providers, and regulators are pushing their use for better component transparency.

Secure Build Pipelines and Provenance (SLSA Framework)

Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts (SLSA, pronounced “salsa”) is a framework that provides stepwise hardening guidelines for build systems. The goal is to prevent tampering at build and deployment time. For example, SLSA Level 2+ recommends generating a signed provenance attestation for each build, so consumers can verify the software was built in a secure, predictable environment and not modified post-build. In practice, this involves measures like using isolated, ephemeral build servers, verifying source control integrity, and signing artifacts. Google, GitHub, and others provide SLSA-compliant build tools (e.g. slsa-github-generator) to help produce these attestations. By incrementally adopting SLSA levels, organizations can dramatically reduce the chance of build pipeline compromises, as was advocated in the wake of incidents like SolarWinds. Complementary to SLSA, projects like in-toto and Sigstore/cosign enable cryptographic signing and verification of software components and container images, ensuring that only trusted, untampered artifacts make it into production.

OpenSSF Scorecards and Best Practices

The Open Source Security Foundation (OpenSSF) has developed a Security Scorecard project to automatically assess open-source projects on a range of security best practices. Scorecards check for things like use of branch protection, dependency updates, signed releases, and whether maintainers enforce MFA – effectively a hygiene rating for open-source components. A high Scorecard score can signal that a project follows good security practices (e.g. the project uses automated testing, has a security policy, and signs its releases with provenance attestations, which maps to SLSA compliance). Organizations are increasingly using such tools to vet the open-source libraries they incorporate. In addition, best-practice frameworks like NIST’s Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF) have been widely adopted. The SSDF defines fundamental secure coding and supply chain practices (such as threat modeling, dependency scanning, code review, and integrity verification) that software producers should follow. Many companies now require their developers and suppliers to align with SSDF or similar guidelines, and federal contracts in the U.S. are beginning to mandate attestation to these practices.

Dependency Monitoring and Software Composition Analysis (SCA)

Given the heavy use of third-party code, organizations are deploying SCA tools (such as GitHub Dependabot, Snyk, Black Duck, etc.) to continuously scan for known vulnerabilities in open-source dependencies. This practice is essential since studies show 84% of codebases contain at least one known open-source vulnerability. SCA tools alert teams when a new CVE is reported in a library they use, and often can even auto-create patches or pull-requests to update the affected package. Combined with SBOMs, this provides an “early warning system” for vulnerable components. Regular patching and rapid update cycles (e.g. using automated dependency update pipelines) are now considered a baseline defense to shorten the window of exposure.

Enhanced Identity and Access Security

Because attackers often start by stealing credentials (developer GitHub accounts, CI tokens, etc.), enforcing strong authentication and identity security is a critical mitigation. In 2023, platforms like GitHub rolled out mandatory two-factor authentication (2FA) for all code contributors. This move is directly aimed at reducing account takeovers that lead to supply chain incidents. As GitHub noted, developer accounts are frequent targets for social engineering, so improving account security “is the first and most critical step toward securing the supply chain”. Similarly, package registries (npm, PyPI) now require 2FA for maintainers of popular packages, and many organizations are implementing strict controls on code-signing keys, such as using hardware security modules (HSMs) or cloud KMS services to prevent unauthorized code signing. By hardening developer accounts, API keys, and build agent credentials (and rotating secrets regularly), companies can shut common entry points used in recent attacks.

Continuous Monitoring and Threat Detection

Given that no defense is foolproof, leading organizations are also investing in detective controls focused on the software supply chain. This includes monitoring source code repositories for unauthorized changes (e.g. unexpected commits to critical dependencies), scanning build outputs for anomalies or known malware, and validating the integrity of binaries before deployment. Advanced runtime threat detection can also catch malicious behavior from a compromised component (for instance, an application suddenly spawning an unexpected process or making abnormal network calls). As one security CEO advised, businesses must shift from “blind trust” in software to using tools that “scan raw code and compiled binaries for unexplained changes that may indicate malware”. For example, in the 3CX incident, some security firms detected the trojanized DLL in 3CX’s app by comparing it against known-good baselines. Behavioral analytics – looking for supply chain attack hallmarks like software contacting unusual domains or processes injecting into others – are now part of many security operations playbooks.

Incident Response Planning and Supplier Risk Management

Mitigation also extends beyond technical measures into process and governance. Organizations are establishing clear policies for evaluating and approving third-party software, including security reviews or requiring suppliers to provide security documentation. Contracts with software vendors increasingly include clauses on timely patching, vulnerability disclosure, and incident response cooperation. It’s also crucial to prepare for the worst: having an incident response plan that involves key suppliers. For instance, if a critical library you use is found compromised, do you know whom to contact, how to get an emergency patch, and how to communicate to your customers? Tabletop exercises now frequently include supply-chain breach scenarios. Continuous audits and supplier assessments (asking vendors about their SDLC security, requiring certifications like ISO 27001, or proof of practices like SBOMs) help maintain accountability. In short, organizations are moving to actively govern their software supply chain as a risk domain, much like they do finance or operations.

No single tool or framework is a silver bullet – it’s the “trifecta” of SBOM transparency, SLSA-style integrity, and Scorecard-driven best practices that together raise the bar. When implemented in layers, these measures make it significantly harder for attackers to slip malicious code into the pipeline unnoticed, and easier for defenders to catch issues early. The result is a more resilient supply chain that can withstand the ever-increasing assaults.

Emerging Trends and Developments (2024–2025)

Software supply chain security is a fast-evolving field. In 2024–2025, several key trends and new developments are shaping how organizations approach this challenge:

Attack Volume and Attacker Profile

The volume of supply chain attacks continues to climb to record highs. For example, malicious packages on open-source registries doubled from 2022 to 2023, and overall software supply chain attacks are projected to cost the global economy $80+ billion by 2026. Notably, the attacker profile has broadened. What was once the realm of nation-state APTs has trickled down to cybercriminal gangs. Ransomware groups, for instance, now see supply chain exploits as high-yield opportunities (witness the Clop gang’s opportunistic exploitation of the MOVEit flaw in 2023). The ease of launching certain attacks – like publishing a malicious npm module – means lower-skilled actors can inflict outsized damage. At the same time, state-backed hackers remain extremely active, often leveraging supply chain vectors for espionage and financial gain (e.g. North Korea’s Lazarus Group targeting software companies and cryptocurrency platforms). We should expect attackers to keep gravitating to whatever supply chain vectors offer the best ROI and stealth, whether that’s open-source poisoning, vendor breaches, or CI/CD compromises.

Greater Adoption of SBOMs and VEX

SBOM usage has moved from theory to practice. Many software vendors now publish SBOMs for their products, and new regulations are solidifying this trend. In the U.S., a federal rule in 2024 will require government software suppliers to provide SBOMs and self-attestations of secure development, pushing SBOMs into the mainstream procurement process. Likewise, the EU’s proposed Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) will effectively mandate SBOMs (or similar component transparency) for a wide range of products sold in Europe. To complement SBOMs, the industry is introducing VEX (Vulnerability Exploitability Exchange) documents – which indicate whether known vulnerabilities in a product are actually exploitable or not in that context, so that teams can prioritize fixes. The overall trend is toward greater transparency about what’s inside software and proactive sharing of security information. Organizations are beginning to integrate SBOM data into their asset databases and vulnerability management tools. Over time, we expect automatic SBOM generation and consumption to become a standard DevSecOps practice, much like automated testing – enabling faster reactions to newly discovered risks in the supply chain.

Regulatory Pressure and Liability Shifts

Governments worldwide are waking up to supply chain security as a systemic risk and are enacting new rules. In the EU, major regulations like NIS2, DORA, and the CRA are coming into force between 2024–2025, which shift cybersecurity from optional best-practice to legal obligation. These rules demand that organizations (particularly in critical sectors) implement supply chain risk management, report incidents, and hold suppliers to higher standards. In the United States, regulators are also sharpening their teeth: the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) took the unprecedented step of suing the SolarWinds CISO in 2023, alleging failures in duty to prevent its 2020 breach – a sign that companies may face liability for inadequate supply chain security. There are increasing calls for software liability so that software producers can be held accountable for negligence or “unsafe” products. While the legal frameworks are still evolving, the direction is clear: organizations must prove their software is built and maintained securely, or face regulatory and legal consequences. This is prompting boards and executives to invest more in software supply chain security, viewing it not just as a technical issue but a compliance and business risk. Efforts like CISA’s new Secure-by-Design guidelines advocate pushing the burden of security upstream onto developers and vendors, rather than end-users.

Security Improvements in Open-Source Ecosystems

The open-source community and ecosystem platforms have introduced significant security improvements. One major development was GitHub’s 2023 mandate of 2FA for all contributors (and a similar push by GitLab and others) to reduce the epidemic of account takeovers. Package managers are increasingly adopting protective measures: PyPI now requires verification for critical package maintainers, npm has enhanced malware scanning and is experimenting with “trust scoring” for packages. Projects like Sigstore (for free code signing of packages using a public transparency log) are gaining momentum, with languages like Python and Kubernetes tooling integrating Sigstore for package verification. Additionally, initiatives such as OpenSSF’s Alpha-Omega Project are funding security audits and improvements for the most critical open-source projects. We are also seeing the rise of automated risk scoring for components (for example, services that score a library based on factors like maintainer reputation, update frequency, known vulns, etc.). All these efforts are gradually strengthening the open-source supply chain by raising the cost for attackers. However, challenges remain: many open-source projects are under-resourced, and attackers will keep probing for soft targets (e.g. uploading malicious code to less-monitored repositories). But overall, community awareness of supply chain threats is at an all-time high, and collaborative initiatives to secure common infrastructure (package registries, build systems) are a positive trend.

Emerging Tech: AI and Supply Chain Security

New technologies like artificial intelligence are beginning to play a dual role. On one hand, defenders are leveraging AI/ML to detect anomalies in code and dependencies – for instance, research has been done on using large language models to identify potentially malicious code in npm packages. AI can help analyze the huge volumes of packages and flag those that behave suspiciously (e.g. an npm package that suddenly starts dropping trojans). On the other hand, attackers might use AI to discover zero-days or write polymorphic malicious code that evades detection. We haven’t yet seen widespread examples of AI-driven supply chain attacks, but experts caution that as AI tools become common in development, ensuring the security of AI-generated code and models will be crucial (especially verifying that AI hasn’t introduced insecure dependencies or logic). Another emerging technology area is blockchain for supply chain integrity – for example, using distributed ledgers to record component hashes and attestations immutably. Some companies are exploring this for critical software used in finance and defense, though it’s not mainstream. Overall, the next wave of tech (AI, blockchain, etc.) is being closely watched in the context of supply chain security, with an eye to harnessing the benefits for defense while mitigating any new risks they introduce.

In summary, the 2024–2025 period is seeing greater momentum than ever in shoring up supply chain security. Attacks are more frequent and varied, but so are the industry’s responses – from regulatory mandates and better tooling to cultural shifts in how software is built (emphasizing security from the design phase onward). Supply chain security has evolved from a niche concern a few years ago to a top-line item in security strategies today, reflecting both the urgency of recent incidents and the promise of new solutions to address them.

Open-Source vs Proprietary: Differences in Risks and Responses

A long-running debate in security is whether open-source software (OSS) or proprietary software is “safer.” In truth, neither model is inherently immune to supply chain threats. Both open and closed-source software have been compromised via supply chain attacks, and modern applications often include a mix of both. The focus in 2024 is therefore less on the licensing model and more on how well the code – open or closed – is managed and secured throughout its life cycle. That said, there are some distinctions in risks and typical responses in the two ecosystems:

Open-Source Ecosystem Risks

The openness of OSS is double-edged. On one hand, having source code publicly available can facilitate community scrutiny and rapid patching when vulnerabilities are found. On the other hand, that openness also allows attackers to study code for weaknesses and, in some cases, to contribute malicious code. Popular open-source projects become massive “upstream” targets – a single compromise can impact thousands of downstream applications. For example, an attacker who gained maintainership of the event-stream npm package in 2018 added malware that stole cryptocurrency wallets, affecting all projects depending on that library. Trust and identity are therefore critical in OSS: an attacker may attempt to impersonate a maintainer (hence GitHub’s 2FA requirement) or exploit a maintainer’s lax security to hijack their account. Another risk unique to open-source is the concept of “dependency confusion,” where an attacker publishes an internal package name to a public registry so that automated build systems pull the malicious public version – a technique demonstrated by researchers in 2021 and later used maliciously. Open-source also tends to have very deep transitive dependencies; a project may pull in hundreds of indirect libraries, so a vulnerability or malicious code in any one of them can slip through unnoticed. Furthermore, many open-source projects are volunteer-run and may not have formal security processes, which can lead to slower response on patches or lack of thorough security testing.

Proprietary Software Risks

Proprietary software typically doesn’t allow public code access, which can make it harder for outsiders (including attackers) to discover vulnerabilities – but it also means users must trust the vendor’s internal security. When proprietary vendors get compromised, the resulting supply chain incident can be widespread because customers have limited ability to detect a malicious update. The SolarWinds Orion backdoor (2020) is a prime example: customers had no visibility into Orion’s source code and thus had to rely entirely on SolarWinds’ update integrity, which was unfortunately subverted by attackers. Proprietary software supply chain attacks often involve infiltrating the vendor’s network, as with 3CX (2023) or CCleaner (2017), and inserting malware into a signed software update. These attacks tend to be stealthy and targeted – often orchestrated by nation-states – aiming to breach specific high-value organizations via the trusted software. Another risk in closed-source environments is hidden open-source usage – since even proprietary software frequently includes open-source components (the average proprietary product might be 70% OSS by volume), customers may not know they are exposed to a given open-source vulnerability unless the vendor discloses it. Without an SBOM, users of proprietary software might remain in the dark about components like Log4j inside a product, delaying their response to vulnerabilities.

Differences in Responses

In the open-source world, the response to supply chain risks has focused on community initiatives and tooling – e.g., the OpenSSF mobilizing companies and developers to improve critical projects, package registries tightening security controls, and developers adopting tools like DependaBot and 2FA. Open-source projects can integrate security fixes quickly, but ensuring all downstream users update is a challenge (there is no centralized push mechanism as with a vendor update). In contrast, proprietary software vendors often have dedicated security teams and release management, so they may respond with out-of-band patches or recalls of compromised updates (for example, 3CX quickly published a clean app update once the compromise was discovered). However, proprietary vendors can also be less transparent – some might initially downplay incidents or be slow to share details, which can hamper customers’ mitigation efforts (the Okta case exposed frustration when the company’s delayed disclosure left clients guessing). Increasingly, the line between OSS and proprietary is blurring: companies build products on open-source, and open-source projects are adopting corporate best practices. This is why regulations like the EU’s NIS2 don’t differentiate open vs closed source in obligations, focusing instead on outcomes like having proper risk management and incident handling regardless of the development model.

Transparency and Trust

One notable difference is the visibility into code. With OSS, anyone can audit the code – and many firms now do so before using a critical library (or hire third-party audits). For proprietary software, customers often rely on certifications or external assessments (like a SOC 2 report or government evaluations) to gain confidence, since they cannot review the code themselves. In terms of trust, open-source relies on a decentralized trust model (web of trust among maintainers and users), whereas proprietary software places trust in the vendor organization. Supply chain security efforts reflect this: open-source efforts focus on maintainer education, digital signing of packages, and community policing of ecosystems, whereas proprietary software efforts focus on secure development practices within companies, vendor security compliance, and customer contractual requirements.

In the end, the security of any software supply chain – open or closed – hinges on maintaining integrity, transparency, and rapid response to issues. As one expert noted, it’s no longer about asking “which is safer, open source or proprietary?” – instead, “the real question is: How well do you manage your software supply chain?” Modern best practices (SBOMs, secure builds, continuous monitoring) apply equally to both models. Both open-source projects and commercial vendors are being held to higher standards by users and regulators. And importantly, both need to collaborate – for instance, a proprietary software maker must track and contribute back to the open-source components it uses, and open-source maintainers benefit from vendor support and funding to improve security. The focus going forward is on process maturity, accountability, and trust across the entire ecosystem, rather than the old open vs closed dichotomy.

Industry-Specific Considerations

Certain industries face unique supply chain security challenges or regulatory requirements due to the nature of their operations and threat exposure. Below are a few industry-specific perspectives for sectors often in the spotlight:

Government and Defense

Governments have been prime targets of supply chain attacks, as adversaries seek to breach agencies by compromising the software they use. The SolarWinds incident (which impacted U.S. federal agencies) was a wake-up call that prompted sweeping changes. Government procurement is now driving stricter requirements for software suppliers. In the U.S., federal agencies (as of 2024) will only procure software from vendors who comply with secure development standards (NIST SSDF) and provide artefacts like SBOMs and security attestations for their products. OMB guidelines and agency mandates are enforcing this, effectively using the government’s buying power to raise the security bar. Similarly, defense contractors are subject to initiatives like the DoD’s Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) which include supply chain risk controls. Government organizations are also adopting a “zero trust” architecture, which extends to software – assuming software may be compromised and implementing continuous verification and strict network segmentation around it (so a tainted application can’t freely access all data). On the international front, the EU NIS2 Directive (effective 2024) requires EU member states’ governments and critical sectors to implement supply chain risk management policies, and to assess the cybersecurity of suppliers (including foreign software). Governments are also sharing threat intelligence specific to software supply chain threats; for example, CISA regularly publishes alerts on active supply chain attacks and recommended mitigations. In summary, the public sector is both improving its own defenses and using regulatory levers to push better security across the software industry. The high stakes (e.g., critical infrastructure or intelligence data at risk) mean that this sector often sets the precedent that others follow.

Financial Services

Banks, insurance companies, and other financial institutions have long histories of stringent risk management, and that extends to IT and supply chain security. This sector deals with highly sensitive data and is heavily regulated. Frameworks such as the PCI-DSS (for payment systems) and guidelines from financial regulators require robust third-party risk management. For instance, banks must maintain an inventory of third-party technology providers and assess their security controls regularly. The new EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) specifically focuses on ICT third-party risk for finance, mandating that banks have contracts and oversight in place for software providers and that critical service providers undergo audits. In practice, large financial firms often perform their own source code reviews or penetration tests on high-risk vendor software and demand the right to do so in contracts. Many have “approved software” lists vendors must be on. Despite this rigor, finance is still a prime target: supply chain attacks here might aim for a foothold to pivot into a bank’s network (as occurred in the NotPetya outbreak of 2017, where malware delivered via an accounting software update affected Maersk, Merck, and also several banks). Cryptocurrency and fintech companies represent a newer frontier – often less regulated than traditional banks, but holding valuable assets. As an example, in February 2025, hackers (attributed to North Korea) pulled off the largest crypto theft to date (~$1.5B in ETH) by breaching a third-party wallet platform used by the Bybit exchange. This was a highly sophisticated supply chain attack that exploited the fintech’s reliance on an outside software provider. The lesson in finance is that adversaries will seek out the weakest link in the chain – whether it’s a small fintech’s software or a widely used banking application – so the sector must keep extending its risk management to cover all suppliers, big and small. We see financial regulators in 2024 increasing oversight of cloud service providers and software vendors, recognizing that a failure in one of these can have systemic impacts (for example, a major core banking software bug or breach could affect many banks at once).

Healthcare and Medical Devices

Healthcare faces the dual challenge of protecting patient data and ensuring patient safety. Hospitals and healthcare providers use a vast array of software and devices (from electronic health record systems to networked infusion pumps), often from niche vendors who may not have strong security practices historically. A supply chain incident in healthcare can literally be life-threatening if it affects critical medical devices or hospital operations. Recognizing this, regulators have stepped in. In the U.S., the FDA in 2023 started enforcing cybersecurity requirements for medical devices: any new device submission must include an extensive cybersecurity plan, including an SBOM of all software components and plans for patching known vulnerabilities. As of October 2023, the FDA can even refuse to approve devices that lack this documentation. This is a landmark shift – it essentially brings supply chain security (via SBOMs and vulnerability management) into the product safety realm for medical tech. In broader healthcare IT, laws like HIPAA mandate protection of patient information, which extends to ensuring third-party software handling that data is secure. The sector has seen breaches through software supply chain vectors: e.g., the 2023 MOVEit breach affected numerous health insurance companies and lab services, exposing millions of patients’ data. Another challenge is that hospitals often run legacy systems (older software, sometimes out of support) due to device compatibility or certification constraints, which increases vulnerability. Industry groups in healthcare are now collaborating on “ingredient list” SBOM sharing for critical software so that if a new threat (say, a library vulnerability) arises, healthcare IT teams can quickly identify affected devices or applications. There’s also emphasis on incident drills – for example, how to continue care operations if a major software supplier is compromised and must be taken offline. Healthcare CISOs report that supply chain attacks are among their top concerns, given the potential impact and the difficulty of securing a long tail of vendors. The silver lining is that awareness is much improved, and initiatives like the FDA’s guidance are forcing better security by design in products that go into hospitals and clinics.

Technology and Cloud Services

Tech companies themselves (including cloud service providers, SaaS vendors, and software firms) are both targets and enablers in the supply chain. A compromise of a major cloud or software platform can ripple out widely (sometimes termed a “mega supply chain” risk). For example, if an attacker found a way to compromise an update to a popular operating system or development tool, the blast radius could be enormous. Accordingly, leading tech firms are at the forefront of adopting supply chain security measures: all major cloud providers have rolled out tools for customers like artifact registries with integrity checks, dependency scanning services, and even managed SBOM services. These companies often publish transparency reports and participate actively in OpenSSF efforts to secure upstream projects. Yet, tech companies are targeted relentlessly by APTs exactly because of their pivotal role. Microsoft, for instance, revealed in 2023 that a North Korean group (Lazarus) had breached a third-party software vendor (CyberLink) and trojanized its installer to infect downstream systems – an attack that Microsoft’s threat intelligence caught and attributed. Incidents like that keep the industry vigilant. Large platform providers have started bug bounty programs specifically for supply chain vulnerabilities, encouraging researchers to find novel attacks like dependency confusion or package planting affecting their ecosystems. Another development is customer pressure: enterprise customers now routinely ask SaaS and software vendors to provide SBOMs and prove their secure development practices as part of vendor risk assessments. This is effectively turning into a market driver for security – a SaaS company that cannot demonstrate supply chain security may lose business to a competitor that can. We also see tech companies forming alliances (like the Linux Foundation’s initiatives) to fund security work in critical open-source components that all of them rely on (for example, funding the OpenSSL project post-Heartbleed, or more recently, the Log4j coordination). The tech industry’s challenge is to not only secure its own direct products but to uplift the security of the entire ecosystem it depends on – a role many are increasingly accepting through collective action.

(Other industries such as energy, manufacturing, and transportation also face supply chain threats – for instance, industrial control systems running on vulnerable software – but the themes are similar: regulatory push for securing supplier products, the potential for physical consequences, and collaborative initiatives (like automotive ISACs sharing SBOM information for vehicle software). Each industry is assessing which parts of its software supply chain are most mission-critical and focusing security efforts there.)

Real-World Case Studies (2023–2025 Incidents)

To illustrate the risks and responses discussed, here are several high-profile software supply chain security incidents from 2023–2025 and the lessons learned from each:

CyberLink Software Compromise (2023)

In late 2023, Microsoft reported that North Korea’s Lazarus Group breached CyberLink, a Taiwanese software company, and trojanized one of its software installers on the official update server. The attackers even signed the malicious installer with CyberLink’s legitimate digital certificate, so it would be trusted by Windows. Dozens of customers who downloaded what appeared to be a legitimate update were infected. Lesson: Even well-known software vendors can have their update mechanism subverted. This case demonstrated the importance of certificate trust lists and revocation – Microsoft swiftly added the abused CyberLink certificate to its disallowed list to prevent further installs. It also underscores that organizations should validate software signatures and perhaps monitor network traffic after software updates (in this case, the trojanized app began beaconing to unusual domains). Code signing alone isn’t a panacea if an attacker steals the signing key; complementary monitoring is needed.

3CX Double Supply Chain Attack (2023)

One of the most notable attacks of 2023 was the compromise of 3CX, a popular VoIP/telephony software provider. In March 2023, 3CX’s desktop app update was found to contain malware (a DLL that harvested data from user machines). Investigation revealed an alarming chain: months earlier, the company’s network was breached when a developer downloaded a poisoned copy of X_Trader, a trading software, from its vendor’s site. North Korean hackers had backdoored X_Trader, which then gave them a foothold in 3CX’s environment – allowing them to corrupt 3CX’s own application build. It was a supply chain attack (Trading Technologies) that led to another supply chain attack (3CX), a rare “double” compromise. The 3CX incident impacted hundreds of thousands of corporate users who trusted its software. Lesson: This demonstrated the devastating potential of transitive trust – your supplier’s supplier can compromise you. Organizations must extend threat modeling to include these indirect dependencies. From a defense view, 3CX highlights the need for runtime anomaly detection: some security firms caught the malicious activity when the trojaned 3CX app started making anomalous connections. Network egress filtering (preventing applications from freely contacting the internet) could mitigate damage. For vendors, the lesson was to verify the integrity of any external software integrated into build systems and to monitor build logs and artifact hashes for unexpected changes. This attack has accelerated interest in SLSA Level 3+ hardened build pipelines, where build processes are isolated and all dependencies are cryptographically verified to prevent such infiltration.

MOVEit Mass Data Breach (2023)

In May–June 2023, a zero-day vulnerability (SQL injection) in MOVEit Transfer, a widely used file transfer software, was exploited by a cybercriminal group (Clop) to steal data from organizations worldwide. This was not a classic “injected backdoor” but a supply chain incident in the sense that many organizations across finance, government, and education all relied on the same third-party software – and a single flaw in it led to breaches in over 1,000 organizations. Personal data of at least 62 million individuals was exposed, including records from multiple U.S. federal agencies and prominent companies. Lesson: A critical lesson here is the importance of third-party patching speed and contingency planning. Progress Software (the vendor) released a patch once the attack came to light, but many organizations were already compromised in the short window. Those with compensating controls (like web application firewalls or network segmentation around the file transfer server) fared better. The incident propelled efforts to inventory “Where do we use MOVEit or similar software?” – again, something facilitated by having SBOMs and an internal software catalog. It also demonstrated why continuous monitoring of critical suppliers (in this case, watching threat intel for any exploits in software you use) is vital. From a regulatory perspective, the MOVEit incident led to heightened scrutiny – for example, several governments demanded reports from any vendors using MOVEit, and breach notification letters went out widely. Companies learned to question the security posture of the software in their stack that might not be headline applications but still handle sensitive data.

Okta Support System Breach (2023)

In October 2023, identity management provider Okta revealed that hackers had accessed its customer support case management system using a stolen credential. Nearly 200 Okta customer organizations’ information (including session tokens or HAR files with authentication data) were affected. This was essentially a supply chain issue: customers of Okta were put at risk due to a breach in Okta’s own support tools. Notably, this followed a similar subprocessor breach Okta had in 2022, making it a repeat scenario. Lesson: The Okta case underscores the need for strong security of support and outsourced services, especially for SaaS providers. It highlighted the expectation that a vendor not only secure its product but also its back-end and support infrastructure with equal rigor (e.g., requiring MFA for support engineers, monitoring support tools for suspicious access). Cloudflare’s team (an Okta customer) publicly criticized Okta’s incident response, urging faster notification and basic security “table stakes” like hardware security keys for all support access. A positive outcome is that many organizations, after Okta’s breach, revisited their access policies – e.g., ensuring that if a third-party provider is breached, the impact is limited (by limiting the data they can access, using client-side encryption, etc.). It’s a reminder that your security is only as strong as that of the vendors and partners with privileged access to your systems.

Bybit Cryptocurrency Heist (2025)

In February 2025, a record-breaking $1.5 billion theft of cryptocurrency occurred, targeting the Bybit exchange. This heist was executed through a supply chain compromise of Safe{Wallet}, a third-party multi-signature wallet platform that Bybit used for managing large transfers. Attackers (North Korea’s Lazarus Group, according to the FBI) social-engineered a Safe{Wallet} developer, planted malicious code on the wallet application’s interface, and covertly altered the destination of a routine funds transfer from Bybit’s cold wallet. Because the transaction details were manipulated in the GUI only after signatures, Bybit staff didn’t realize the funds were being siphoned to the attackers’ addresses until it was too late. Lesson: This highly sophisticated attack showcases targeted supply chain manipulation – the adversary studied the operational workflow (multisig transfers) and the specific third-party software in use, then crafted a pinpoint exploit. It reinforces that in critical industries like crypto, you must verify the integrity of not just code but also transaction outputs (e.g., using out-of-band verification of destination addresses). For any business, the Bybit case is a caution that your “crown jewel” processes might rely on external software that itself becomes a single point of failure. It also highlights the growing trend of attackers focusing on lucrative sectors (here, crypto) and the importance of insider threat training (a simple phish of a developer led to this multi-stage operation). The fallout has spurred exchanges and fintech firms to audit the security of their suppliers more thoroughly and consider diversification or additional checks when using third-party platforms for critical transactions.

These case studies drive home a common theme: an organization’s security is intertwined with the security of its software supply chain. Each incident provided painful lessons that have influenced the industry’s approach – from adopting new tools and policies to engaging in greater information sharing about threats. As we’ve seen, whether the attack came via an open-source package, a proprietary update, or a service provider, the organizations that fared best were those prepared with layered defenses and swift response plans. Real-world incidents have served as catalysts for many of the improvements discussed earlier (such as SBOM adoption and regulatory changes).

Conclusion

Software supply chain security has become a strategic priority by 2025, evolving from niche concern to core security practice. The current landscape is defined by high stakes: attackers can compromise thousands of victims in one go by exploiting trust in the software ecosystem, while defenders are tasked with safeguarding an increasingly complex web of dependencies and third-party code. The historical divide between open-source and proprietary software matters less than the maturity of processes and transparency around each. As experts note, securing the supply chain is “not just about the code – it’s about processes, monitoring, trust, and accountability at every stage”.

The momentum is toward proactive governance of software supply chains. Organizations are moving from reactive patching and incident response to building security into every link: vetting suppliers, implementing secure development pipelines, continuously monitoring for anomalies, and having contingency plans for when (not if) something goes wrong. Regulations and industry standards are reinforcing this shift, ensuring that software producers and consumers alike share responsibility for raising the security baseline.

In conclusion, while the challenge is formidable, the advances in the last two years offer reason for optimism. Through a combination of better tools (like SBOMs and SLSA), collaborative efforts (open-source security initiatives), and smarter oversight (both internal and regulatory), the software industry is gradually fortifying its supply chain. The key lesson is that trust must be earned and verified at every step – from the moment code is written to the moment it’s running in production. By embracing that mindset, the community can stay ahead of adversaries and keep our software ecosystem worthy of the confidence we place in it every day.
